These lecture notes provide a comprehensive overview of the mathematical formulation of numerical relativity, beginning with a physical review of Newtonian gravity and General Relativity (GR) before introducing the 3+1 decomposition.

## 1. Course Introduction and Logistics

The course focuses on developing numerical methods to solve Einstein’s field equations.

* 
**Notation:** A superscript "4" (e.g., ${^4}\Gamma$, ${^4}R$) is used to distinguish four-dimensional space-time objects from the three-dimensional spatial objects introduced later in the 3+1 formalism.



---

## 2. Newtonian Gravity: The Conceptual Template

Newtonian gravity serves as a structural guide for developing the more complex framework of General Relativity.

| Feature | Newtonian Description |
| --- | --- |
| **Central Object** | The gravitational potential $\phi$.

 |
| **Particle Motion** | Freely falling particles experience an acceleration $a_i = -\partial_i \phi$ (the gradient of the potential).

 |
| **Geodesic Deviation** | The distance between nearby falling objects involves the **second derivatives** of $\phi$.

 |
| **Field Equation** | The Poisson equation: $\nabla^2 \phi = 4\pi \rho$ (the trace of the second derivatives).

 |
| **Gauge Freedom** | Physical results remain unchanged if a constant is added to the potential ($\phi \rightarrow \phi + C$).

 |

---

## 3. General Relativity: Mathematical Foundations

In GR, gravity is described through the curvature of space-time rather than a force.

### 3.1 The Central Object: The Metric

The space-time metric $g_{ab}$ is used to measure proper distances $ds$ between events:


$$ds^2 = g_{ab} dx^a dx^b$$



### 3.2 Curvature and Parallel Transport

Curvature is measured by observing how vectors change when moved "parallel" to themselves along a curve.

* 
**Covariant Derivative ($\nabla_a$):** Generalizes the partial derivative so the result remains a tensor.


* 
**Christoffel Symbols ($\Gamma^a_{bc}$):** Connection coefficients associated with the metric.


* 
**Geodesic Equation:** Freely falling objects follow paths where their four-velocity $u^a$ is parallel transported along itself:



$$u^a \nabla_a u^b = 0$$


This involves **first derivatives** of the metric, analogous to the Newtonian gradient.



### 3.3 The Riemann and Ricci Tensors

* 
**Riemann Tensor ($R^a_{bcd}$):** Represents the second derivatives of the metric and measures curvature via geodesic deviation. It has $\frac{1}{12}n^2(n^2-1)$ independent components.


* 
**Ricci Tensor ($R_{ab}$):** The only non-trivial trace of the Riemann tensor ($R_{ab} = R^c_{acb}$).


* 
**Ricci Scalar ($R$):** The trace of the Ricci tensor ($R = g^{ab} R_{ab}$).



---

## 4. Einstein’s Field Equations

The field equations relate the geometry of space-time to its energy and matter content.


$$G_{ab} = 8\pi T_{ab}$$



* 
**Einstein Tensor ($G_{ab}$):** Defined as $R_{ab} - \frac{1}{2}g_{ab}R$.


* 
**Stress-Energy Tensor ($T_{ab}$):** Describes the density of energy, momentum, and stress.


* 
**Vacuum Case:** In the absence of matter, the equations reduce to $R_{ab} = 0$, though the full Riemann curvature may still be non-zero.


* 
**Cosmological Constant ($\Lambda$):** Typically assumed to be zero in numerical relativity because the scales of interest (e.g., black hole mergers) are much smaller than cosmological scales.



---

## 5. Analytical Solutions

### 5.1 Weak Field Limit

By perturbing the flat Minkowski metric ($g_{ab} = \eta_{ab} + h_{ab}$), the field equations can be linearized into a wave equation:

* 
**Quasi-static solutions:** Recover Newtonian gravity.


* 
**Dynamical solutions:** Predict the existence of gravitational waves.



### 5.2 Spherical Symmetry and Stellar Models

* 
**Schwarzschild Solution:** The unique vacuum solution for a spherically symmetric mass. It features an event horizon at $r = 2M$.


* 
**Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) Equations:** Describe the hydrostatic equilibrium of a relativistic star.


* 
**Equation of State (EOS):** A relationship between pressure $p$ and density $\rho^*$ must be provided to solve for a stellar model.


* 
**Matching:** A complete stellar space-time is constructed by integrating fluid equations from the center to the surface ($p=0$) and matching the result to the exterior Schwarzschild solution.



---

## 6. Introduction to the 3+1 Decomposition

To solve Einstein's equations numerically, the four-dimensional space-time is "chopped up" or foliated into a family of three-dimensional spatial slices $\Sigma_t$.

* 
**Foliation:** Slices are defined as level surfaces of a scalar time function $t$.


* 
**Normal Vector ($n^a$):** A unit vector normal to the slices, pointing in the direction of increasing $t$:



$$n_a = -\alpha \nabla_a t$$





* 
**Lapse Function ($\alpha$):** A normalization constant that measures the rate of advance of proper time for a normal observer.
