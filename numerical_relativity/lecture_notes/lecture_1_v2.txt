## II. Newtonian Gravity: The Conceptual Blueprint

To understand GR, we first establish a four-step outline in Newtonian gravity that will be mirrored in the relativistic development. 

### 1. The Central Object

The central object of the theory is the **gravitational potential** $\phi$. 

### 2. Motion (First Derivatives)

Freely falling particles experience an acceleration $a$ related to the **first derivative** (gradient) of the potential: 

$$a_i = -\partial_i \phi$$

### 3. Geodesic Deviation (Second Derivatives)

The distance between two nearby freely falling particles involves the **second derivatives** of the potential, often derived via a Taylor expansion. 

### 4. Field Equation (The Trace)

The field equation (Poisson's equation) involves the **trace** of the second derivatives (the Laplace operator): 

$$\nabla^2 \phi = \partial^i \partial_i \phi = 4\pi \rho$$

* 
**Note:** This theory possesses a "gauge freedom" where adding a constant to $\phi$ does not change the physical results (the gradient remains the same).

## III. General Relativity: The Geometric Framework

Following the Newtonian outline, we translate these concepts into the language of four-dimensional curved spacetime. 

### 1. The Central Object: The Metric

The fundamental object is the **spacetime metric** $g_{ab}$, used to measure proper distances ($ds$) between events: 

$$ds^2 = g_{ab} dx^a dx^b$$

### 2. Measuring Curvature

Curvature is measured through: 

* 
**Sum of angles in triangles:** Exceeding 180° in positively curved space. 


* **Parallel Transport:** Moving a vector along a path such that it remains "as parallel as possible." In curved space, parallel transporting a vector around a closed loop results in a vector that does not point in its original direction. 



### 3. The Covariant Derivative & Connection

To generalize the partial derivative so the result remains a tensor, we introduce the **covariant derivative** $\nabla_a$ and the **Christoffel symbols** (connection coefficients) $\Gamma^c_{ab}$: 

$$\nabla_a T^b = \partial_a T^b + \Gamma^b_{ac} T^c$$

The Christoffel symbols are defined by first derivatives of the metric: 

$$\Gamma^a_{bc} = \frac{1}{2} g^{ad} (\partial_c g_{db} + \partial_b g_{dc} - \partial_d g_{bc})$$

* 
**Notation Note:** A "4" index ($^4\Gamma$) is used to distinguish four-dimensional spacetime objects from the three-dimensional spatial objects introduced in later lectures.

## IV. From Motion to Field Equations

### 1. Geodesics (First Derivatives)

Freely falling objects follow **geodesics**, parallel transporting their own four-velocity $u^a$: 

$$u^a \nabla_a u^b = 0 \implies u^a \partial_a u^b + \Gamma^b_{ac} u^a u^c = 0$$

Consistent with the Newtonian model, this involves **first derivatives** of the metric. 

### 2. Riemann Tensor (Second Derivatives)

**Geodesic deviation** (the acceleration between two nearby worldlines) leads to the **Riemann Tensor** $R^a{}_{bcd}$, which consists of **second derivatives** of the metric. 

* It measures the non-commutativity of covariant derivatives: $[\nabla_a, \nabla_b] v^c = R^c{}_{dab} v^d$. 


* In $n$ dimensions, it has $\frac{1}{12} n^2 (n^2 - 1)$ independent components. 



### 3. Ricci Tensor and Scalar (The Trace)

To form field equations, we take the trace of the Riemann tensor to get the **Ricci Tensor** $R_{ab}$: 

$$R_{ab} = R^c{}_{acb}$$

The **Ricci Scalar** is the trace of the Ricci tensor: $R = R^a{}_a$. 

> 
> **Three Ways to Compute the Ricci Tensor:** 1.  As a trace of the Riemann tensor (using $\Gamma$ and its first derivatives). 2.  Directly using second derivatives of the metric (four complex terms). 3.  Using a wave-operator form by absorbing terms into a contracted Christoffel symbol $\Gamma^c$. 
>

## V. Einstein’s Field Equations

Guided by the Newtonian limit, we arrive at the field equations: 

$$G_{ab} = 8\pi T_{ab}$$

* 
**Einstein Tensor ($G_{ab}$):** Defined as $R_{ab} - \frac{1}{2} g_{ab} R$. 


* 
**Stress-Energy Tensor ($T_{ab}$):** Represents energy, momentum, and stress densities. 


* 
**Cosmological Constant ($\Lambda$):** Assumed to be zero for numerical relativity purposes, as it only impacts cosmological scales. 


* 
**Vacuum Solutions:** In vacuum ($T_{ab} = 0$), the equations reduce to $R_{ab} = 0$. 



---

## VI. Analytical Solutions

### 1. Weak Fields

Assuming $g_{ab} = \eta_{ab} + h_{ab}$ (where $h_{ab} \ll 1$), the field equations become a **wave equation**: 

* 
**Quasi-static solutions:** Recover Newtonian gravity. 


* 
**Dynamical solutions:** Predict **Gravitational Waves**. 



### 2. Strong Fields (Spherical Symmetry)

* 
**Schwarzschild Solution:** The unique vacuum solution for a static, spherically symmetric mass $M$. 


* Features an event horizon at the **Schwarzschild radius** $r = 2M$ (a coordinate singularity). 


* Features a physical singularity at $r = 0$. 




* 
**Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) Equations:** Describe the interior of a static star made of a perfect fluid. 


* Requires an **Equation of State** $P(\rho)$ to solve. 


* Stellar models are constructed by integrating from the center until $P=0$, then matching to the exterior Schwarzschild solution.

## VII. The 3+1 Decomposition: The "Sad Moment"

To solve these equations numerically, we must "undo" the 4D unification and treat spacetime as a sequence of 3D spatial slices evolving in time. 

* 
**Foliation:** We carve the 4D manifold into a family of non-intersecting spatial slices $\Sigma$, labeled by a time function $t$. 


* 
**Normal Vector ($n^a$):** A vector orthogonal to the slices, proportional to the gradient of the time function: 


$$n_a = -\alpha \nabla_a t$$


* 
**Lapse Function ($\alpha$):** A normalization constant that measures the lapse of proper time between slices. 


* 
**Normalization:** $n^a n_a = -\epsilon$, where $\epsilon=1$ for Lorentzian spacetime (timelike normal) and $\epsilon=-1$ for Euclidean space.
