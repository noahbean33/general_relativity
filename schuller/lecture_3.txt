These lecture notes cover the fundamental concepts of multilinear algebra as presented in the third lecture of the International Winter School on Gravity and Light. The focus is on transitioning from abstract vector space definitions to the construction of tensors and their components.

## I. Motivation: Why Vector Spaces?

Before diving into the math, it is crucial to understand where these structures fit into the study of gravity and light.

* 
**SpaceTime vs. Vector Spaces**: Physical space or SpaceTime is **not** equipped with a vector space structure.


* 
**The "Paris" Problem**: A vector space requires addition and scalar multiplication; however, notions like "Paris + Vienna" or "$5 \times \text{Paris}$" have no meaning in physical position space.


* 
**The Role of Tangent Spaces**: While the underlying manifold (SpaceTime) is not a vector space, the **tangent spaces** ($T_p M$) attached to each point of a smooth manifold **do** carry a natural vector space structure.


* 
**Abstract Study**: Understanding tensors requires first studying vector spaces abstractly, as they serve as the building blocks for the finite-dimensional tangent spaces used in general relativity.



---

## II. Formal Definition of a Vector Space

A real (or $\mathbb{R}$) vector space is a triple consisting of a set $V$, an addition operation ($+$), and an S-multiplication operation ($\cdot$).

### 1. The Operations

* 
**Addition**: A map $+: V \times V \to V$ that takes two vectors and produces a third.


* 
**S-multiplication**: A map $\cdot: \mathbb{R} \times V \to V$ that takes a real number and a vector to produce a vector.



### 2. The Eight Axioms

To qualify as a vector space, the triple must satisfy eight specific axioms (often abbreviated as **ADU KME**):

* 
**Commutativity (Addition)**: $v + w = w + v$.


* 
**Associativity (Addition)**: $(u + v) + w = u + (v + w)$.


* 
**Neutral Element**: There exists a "circle" (zero vector) $\mathbf{0} \in V$ such that $v + \mathbf{0} = v$ for all $v \in V$.


* 
**Inverse Element**: For every $v \in V$, there exists an element $-v$ such that $v + (-v) = \mathbf{0}$.


* 
**Associativity (Scalar)**: $\lambda \cdot (\mu \cdot v) = (\lambda \cdot \mu) \cdot v$, where $\lambda, \mu \in \mathbb{R}$.


* 
**Distributive Law I**: $(\lambda + \mu) \cdot v = \lambda \cdot v + \mu \cdot v$.


* 
**Distributive Law II**: $\lambda \cdot (v + w) = \lambda \cdot v + \lambda \cdot w$.


* 
**Unitary Law**: $1 \cdot v = v$.



> 
> **A Note on Terminology**: An "element" of a vector space is informally referred to as a "vector". However, an object is only a vector if it belongs to a set that has been explicitly equipped with the addition and multiplication structures described above.

## III. Linear Maps and Homomorphisms

Once spaces are defined, we study the maps that respect their structure.

* 
**Linear Maps**: A map $f: V \to W$ is linear if it preserves addition and scalar multiplication: $f(v + \tilde{v}) = f(v) + f(\tilde{v})$ and $f(\lambda v) = \lambda f(v)$.


* 
**The Set $Hom(V, W)$**: The collection of all linear maps between $V$ and $W$ is itself a vector space.


* 
**Composition**: The composition of two linear maps is always a linear map. This principle underlies the rules of matrix multiplication.



### Example: The Differentiation Operator

If $P$ is a vector space of polynomials, the differentiation operator $\Delta: P \to P$ is a linear map because the derivative of a sum is the sum of the derivatives.

---

## IV. Dual Vector Spaces ($V^*$)

The **dual space** is a critical building block for multilinear algebra.

* 
**Definition**: For a vector space $V$, the dual space $V^*$ is the set of all linear maps from $V$ into the real numbers $\mathbb{R}$.


* 
**Covectors**: Elements of $V^*$ are informally called **covectors**.


* 
**Example (Integration)**: The operation of definite integration (e.g., $\int_{0}^{1} p(x) dx$) is a covector because it linearly maps a polynomial to a real number.


* 
**The Gradient**: In higher dimensions, the gradient of a function is properly understood as a covector, not a vector.



---

## V. Tensors

Tensors are the generalized "higher" structures built from $V$ and $V^*$.

* 
**Definition**: An $(r, s)$ tensor $T$ over $V$ is a **multilinear map** that takes $r$ copies of $V^*$ and $s$ copies of $V$ and maps them to $\mathbb{R}$.



$$T: \underbrace{V^* \times \dots \times V^*}_{r} \times \underbrace{V \times \dots \times V}_{s} \to \mathbb{R}$$


* 
**Multilinearity**: This means the map is linear in **each** slot independently.



### Vectors and Covectors as Tensors

* 
**Covectors** are $(0, 1)$ tensors.


* 
**Vectors** can be considered $(1, 0)$ tensors (specifically in finite dimensions where $V \cong V^{**}$).


* 
**Inner Products**: A standard inner product is a $(0, 2)$ tensor.



---

## VI. Bases and Components

While "gentlemen and ladies" only choose a basis if they must, doing so allows us to represent vectors and tensors as collections of numbers.

### 1. Hamel Basis

A subset $B \subset V$ is a basis if every vector $v \in V$ can be uniquely represented as a **finite** linear combination of elements in $B$.

* 
**Dimension**: The number of elements in a basis.


* 
**Components**: Once a basis $\{e_1, \dots, e_n\}$ is chosen, a vector $v$ can be represented by unique numbers $v^i$ such that $v = v^i e_i$.



### 2. Dual Basis

Once a basis $\{e_a\}$ is chosen for $V$, there is a unique **dual basis** $\{\epsilon^a\}$ for $V^*$ defined by the requirement:


$$\epsilon^a(e_b) = \delta^a_b$$

### 3. Tensor Components

For an $(r, s)$ tensor $T$, the components are found by plugging the basis vectors and covectors into the tensor's slots:


$$T^{i_1 \dots i_r}_{j_1 \dots j_s} = T(\epsilon^{i_1}, \dots, \epsilon^{i_r}, e_{j_1}, \dots, e_{j_s})$$

---

## VII. Einstein Summation Convention

To simplify the "massive arrays of numbers" generated by tensor components, we use Einstein's "invisible sum" sign.

* 
**The Rule**: If an index appears twice—once in the "up" position and once in the "down" position—a summation over that index is implied.


* **Up/Down Convention**:
* 
**Basis vectors** are labeled with indices **down** ($e_i$).


* 
**Dual basis covectors** are labeled with indices **up** ($\epsilon^i$).


* 
**Vector components** go **up** ($v^i$), and **covector components** go **down** ($\phi_i$).
