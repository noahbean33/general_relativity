Lecture 4: Differentiable Manifolds
International Winter School on Gravity and Light 2015

This lecture explores the transition from topological manifolds to differentiable manifolds, focusing on the extra structures required to define physics-essential concepts like velocity and differentiability.

1. The Motivation: Moving Beyond Continuity
While a topological manifold (M,O) allows us to define continuous curves—trajectories that do not "jump"—it is insufficient for most of classical physics. In physics, we need to associate a velocity (a unique tangent vector) to a curve at every point.


The Intuition: A curve with a sharp "edge" or "corner" is continuous but not differentiable; it lacks a unique velocity at the corner.


The Problem: The structure of a topological manifold alone does not provide enough information to distinguish between a "smooth" curve and one with a sharp edge.


The Strategy: To define differentiability on a manifold, we "lift" the undergraduate definition of differentiability from R 
d
  to the manifold using charts.

Apply Calculus: Judge the differentiability of this representative using standard "undergraduate" calculus (e.g., taking component-wise derivatives).

The Issue of "Well-Definedness"
A definition is only "well-defined" if it does not depend on the specific chart chosen by the observer. If we have two overlapping charts (U,x) and (V,y), one might suggest the curve is differentiable while the other does not.

The relationship between the two representatives is governed by the chart transition map: y∘x 
−1

In a standard topological manifold, transition maps are only guaranteed to be continuous.

Because the composition of a differentiable map and a continuous map is not necessarily differentiable, the "undergraduate" notion of differentiability can be destroyed during a chart change.

This presents a profound challenge for physics: since spacetime is four-dimensional, there are uncountably many ways to define differentiability on the same underlying topological space. We cannot simply "let experiment decide" easily when faced with an infinite, uncountable number of mathematical choices.
