Lecture 7: ConnectionsInternational Winter School on Gravity and Light 2015This lecture marks a transition from the "hand-provided" structures of topology and smooth manifolds to the structures that are subject to physical laws, such as Einstein’s equations. While we have previously defined directional derivatives for functions, we now introduce the Connection (or Covariant Derivative) to extend this concept to arbitrary tensor fields.+41. Motivation: Beyond FunctionsSo far, we have used vector fields $X$ to provide directional derivatives of smooth functions $f$ (denoted $Xf$). However, this does not come "for free" when deriving higher-rank tensors.+2The Problem: On a general smooth manifold, there is no automatic way to take the directional derivative of a vector field or a tensor field.+1The Solution: We must introduce an additional structure, the connection $\nabla$, to define how tensors change along a direction.+12. Formal Definition of a ConnectionA connection $\nabla$ on a smooth manifold $M$ is a map that takes a vector field $X$ and a $(p, q)$ tensor field $T$ and yields a new $(p, q)$ tensor field $\nabla_X T$.To be a valid connection, the map must satisfy four primary axioms:Function Rule: For a $(0,0)$ tensor (a function $f$), the connection acts as a standard directional derivative: $\nabla_X f = Xf$.Additivity: It is additive in the tensor fields it acts upon: $\nabla_X (T + S) = \nabla_X T + \nabla_X S$.Leibniz Rule: It follows the product rule for evaluated tensors. For a $(1,1)$ tensor $T(\omega, Y)$:$$\nabla_X (T(\omega, Y)) = (\nabla_X T)(\omega, Y) + T(\nabla_X \omega, Y) + T(\omega, \nabla_X Y)$$.$C^\infty$-Linearity in the Direction: It is linear with respect to the vector field $X$ in the lower slot, meaning the scaling of the direction at a point $P$ only depends on the value at $P$: $\nabla_{fX + Z} T = f\nabla_X T + \nabla_Z T$.+13. Connection Coefficient Functions ($\Gamma$)To perform calculations in a local chart $(U, x)$, we introduce connection coefficient functions (often called Christoffel symbols).+2Definition: The coefficients $\Gamma^i_{jk}$ are defined by the action of the connection on basis vector fields:
$$\nabla_{\frac{\partial}{\partial x^j}} \frac{\partial}{\partial x^k} = \Gamma^i_{kj} \frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}$$+2Key Index Convention: In this course, the last index ($j$ in $\Gamma^i_{kj}$) corresponds to the direction of the derivative.+34. Component Rules for TensorsThe same $\Gamma$ coefficients allow us to derive any tensor field. The general rule is to take the "naive" derivative of the components and then add a correction term for every index:+3Field TypeComponent FormulaNoteVector Field $Y$$(\nabla_X Y)^i = X(Y^i) + \Gamma^i_{nm} Y^n X^m$Plus sign for upper indices.+2Co-vector Field $\omega$$(\nabla_X \omega)_i = X(\omega_i) - \Gamma^n_{im} \omega_n X^m$Minus sign for lower indices.$(1,2)$ Tensor Field $T$See detailed Leibniz expansion in transcript.+1Each index adds/subtracts a $\Gamma$ term.5. Transformation Law and TorsionThe connection coefficients $\Gamma$ are not components of a tensor. When changing from chart $x$ to chart $y$, they transform as:
+1$$\Gamma'^i_{jk} = \frac{\partial y^i}{\partial x^q} \frac{\partial x^s}{\partial y^j} \frac{\partial x^p}{\partial y^k} \Gamma^q_{sp} + \frac{\partial y^i}{\partial x^q} \frac{\partial^2 x^q}{\partial y^j \partial y^k}$$Inhomogeneous Term: The second term (the second derivative of the coordinate transition) means that even if $\Gamma = 0$ in one chart, it may be non-zero in another (e.g., polar coordinates).Torsion: The anti-symmetric part of the connection ($\Gamma^i_{[jk]}$) is a tensor.+1Normal Coordinates: At any single point $P$, you can always construct a chart (a "normal coordinate chart") where the symmetric part of $\Gamma$ vanishes. However, you cannot generally make it vanish in a neighborhood unless the manifold is flat.
