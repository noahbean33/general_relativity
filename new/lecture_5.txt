## 1. Introduction: The Lead Question

The central inquiry of this lecture is: **On a manifold, what is the velocity of a curve $\gamma$ at a point $P$?**. It is essential to distinguish between **velocity** and **speed**:

* 
**Velocity:** A vector defined in the tangent space.


* 
**Speed:** A scalar that cannot be defined until a **metric** is introduced to the manifold.



Students are encouraged to "erase everything" previously learned about velocities to rediscover the concept through an intrinsic, mathematical lens.

---

## 2. Formal Definition of Velocity

Velocity is defined as a **linear map**.

### The Setup

* 
**Manifold:** A smooth manifold $M$ with a smooth atlas.


* 
**Curve:** A curve $\gamma: \mathbb{R} \to M$ that is at least $C^1$ (once continuously differentiable).


* 
**Point $P$:** The curve hits point $P$ at some parameter value $\lambda_0$, such that $\gamma(\lambda_0) = P$.



### The Map

The velocity $V_{\gamma, P}$ is a linear map from the vector space of smooth functions $C^\infty(M)$ to the real numbers $\mathbb{R}$:


$$V_{\gamma, P}: C^\infty(M) \to \mathbb{R}$$

The rule for this map, acting on a function $f$, is defined as the derivative of the composition of $f$ and $\gamma$:


$$V_{\gamma, P}(f) = (f \circ \gamma)'(\lambda_0)$$

> 
> **Intuition:** Velocity is a **directional derivative**. Like a child running through the world feeling a temperature function $f$, the velocity captures the rate of change of that function as they move along the curve.
> 
>

## 3. The Tangent Space ($T_p M$)

The tangent space at a point $P$, denoted $T_p M$, is the **set of all possible velocity vectors** of all possible smooth curves passing through $P$.

### Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic View

Historically, there was a debate on whether to define manifolds and tangent spaces **intrinsically** (within the manifold) or **extrinsically** (embedded in a larger ambient space).

* 
**Extrinsic:** Visualizing a plane touching a sphere.


* 
**Intrinsic:** Defining vectors as linear maps/directional derivatives.
In General Relativity, we prefer the **intrinsic view** because it is uneconomical and physically unnecessary to refer to an "ambient space" outside our own universe.



---

## 4. Vector Space Structure of $T_p M$

The set $T_p M$ can be equipped with addition and scalar multiplication to become a vector space.

### Proof of Closure

For $T_p M$ to be a vector space, the sum of two velocities must itself be the velocity of some third curve:

1. 
**Scalar Multiplication:** Given a velocity $V_{\gamma, P}$ and a scalar $\alpha$, we can construct a new curve $\tau(\lambda) = \gamma(\alpha \lambda)$ whose velocity is $\alpha V_{\gamma, P}$.


2. 
**Addition:** Given $V_{\gamma, P}$ and $V_{\delta, P}$, we construct a curve $\sigma$ (using a chart) such that its velocity is the sum of the two. Although the construction uses a chart, the resulting tangent vector is **chart-independent**.



**Warning:** While velocities form a vector space, **positions** on a manifold do not. Adding position vectors is meaningless in curved spacetime.

---

## 5. Components and the Chart-Induced Basis

To reconcile abstract vectors with calculations, we use a **chart** $(U, x)$.

### Chart-Induced Basis

For a chart $x$, the basis vectors of $T_p M$ are the partial derivatives with respect to the coordinate entries:


$$\left\{ \left. \frac{\partial}{\partial x^1} \right|_P, \dots, \left. \frac{\partial}{\partial x^d} \right|_P \right\}$$

These are often denoted simply as $\partial_i$.

### Vector Components

The components $v^i$ of a velocity vector are the derivatives of the curve's coordinates in the chart:


$$v^i = (x^i \circ \gamma)'(\lambda_0)$$

Any vector $X \in T_p M$ can then be written as:


$$X = X^i \frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}$$

---

## 6. Transformation Laws

While the **vector itself is invariant** (the physical velocity of a bird), its **components change** when we switch from chart $x$ to chart $y$.

| Object | Transformation Formula |
| --- | --- |
| **Vector Components** | <br>$X^j_y = \frac{\partial y^j}{\partial x^i} X^i_x$ 

 |
| **Co-vector Components** | <br>$\omega_{i, y} = \frac{\partial x^j}{\partial y^i} \omega_{j, x}$ 

 |

The matrices representing these transformations are **inverses** of each other.

---

## 7. Cotangent Spaces and Gradients

The **cotangent space** $T^*_p M$ is the dual of the tangent space, consisting of linear maps from $T_p M$ to $\mathbb{R}$.

### The Gradient as a Co-vector

The gradient of a function $f$ (denoted $df$) is a **co-vector** (a 0,1 tensor), not a vector.

* It acts on a vector to produce a real number: $df(X) = X(f)$.


* Its components are the partial derivatives $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x^i}$.


* It transforms via the **chain rule** of standard analysis, which naturally follows the co-vector transformation law.
