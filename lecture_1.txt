Lecture 1: Topological SpacesCourse: International Winter School on Gravity and Light 2015 Topic: The structure of SpaceTime and the mathematical foundation of continuity +11. Introduction: The Physical ContextThe central goal of this lecture series is to define the structure of SpaceTime. In modern physics, particularly General Relativity, SpaceTime is defined by a rigorous mathematical framework:+1Physical Key Definition: SpaceTime is a four-dimensional topological manifold carrying a smooth atlas, a torsion-free connection, a Lorentzian metric, and a time orientation, all satisfying the Einstein equations.The first step in understanding this definition is to move beyond an intuitive notion of "space and time" and establish the weakest mathematical structure that allows for the description of continuity.2. Motivation for TopologyAt its coarsest level, SpaceTime is simply a set (a collection of points). However, a set alone is insufficient for physics because:+1Continuity is Essential: In classical physics, we assume trajectories (curves) do not "jump".Set Structure is Insufficient: A set lacks the necessary tools to determine if a map or curve is continuous.Topology as the Solution: A topology is the weakest structure that can be established on a set to provide a rigorous definition of continuity.3. Definition of a TopologyLet $M$ be a set. A topology $\mathcal{O}$ is a subset of the power set $\mathcal{P}(M)$ (the set of all subsets of $M$) that satisfies three axioms:AxiomRequirementDescriptionI$\emptyset \in \mathcal{O}$ and $M \in \mathcal{O}$The empty set and the entire set must be included.II$U, V \in \mathcal{O} \implies U \cap V \in \mathcal{O}$The intersection of any two sets in the topology must also be in the topology.III$\bigcup_{\alpha \in I} U_\alpha \in \mathcal{O}$The union of an arbitrary (even uncountable) collection of sets in the topology must remain in the topology.Key Terminology:Topological Space: The pair $(M, \mathcal{O})$.Open Set: Any subset $U \subseteq M$ that is an element of the chosen topology $\mathcal{O}$.Closed Set: A subset $A \subseteq M$ whose complement ($M \setminus A$) is an open set.Warning: "Open" and "Closed" are not mutually exclusive; a set can be both, neither, or only one.4. Examples of TopologiesA. Trivial and Extreme CasesChaotic Topology: Consists only of the empty set and the entire set ($\mathcal{O} = \{\emptyset, M\}$).Discrete Topology: Consists of every possible subset of $M$ ($\mathcal{O} = \mathcal{P}(M)$).Note: While these are mathematically valid, they are often "useless" for physical modeling but serve as important extreme test cases.B. The Standard Topology on $\mathbb{R}^d$This is the topology used in undergraduate analysis, defined via "soft balls":Define Soft Balls: $B_r(p) = \{q \in \mathbb{R}^d \mid \sum (q_i - p_i)^2 < r^2\}$.Implicit Definition: A set $U$ is in the standard topology if, for every point $p \in U$, there exists a radius $r > 0$ such that $B_r(p) \subseteq U$.5. Continuous MapsThe primary reason to define a topology is to facilitate the definition of continuous maps.Definition: A map $f: M \to N$ between two topological spaces $(M, \mathcal{O}_M)$ and $(N, \mathcal{O}_N)$ is continuous if the pre-image of every open set in the target space is an open set in the domain.+1Pre-image formula: $f^{-1}(V) = \{m \in M \mid f(m) \in V\}$.Core Principle: "Pre-images of open sets are open".Dependency: Continuity depends entirely on which topologies are chosen for both the domain and the target.+16. Inheriting Topology (The Subspace Topology)If we have a topological space $(M, \mathcal{O}_M)$ and a subset $S \subseteq M$, we can "inherit" a topology for $S$:The Subset Topology ($\mathcal{O}|_S$): A set $V \subseteq S$ is open in the subset topology if it can be written as the intersection of an open set $U \in \mathcal{O}_M$ and $S$ ($V = U \cap S$).Physical Utility: This ensures that if a map (like a temperature distribution on a plate) is continuous on the whole set, its restriction to a subset (like a wire on that plate) remains continuous under the inherited topology.
